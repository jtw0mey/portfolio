    P r o j e c t

    For this project I will be playing the role of an Analytics Consultant hired by the U.S. Department of Energy. I will need to consolidate and clean up raw data, and create a dashboard or report to help understand patterns and trends around power outages in the U.S. between 2002 and 2023.

    I will be using real data provided by the U.S. Department of Energy that is publically available.



    D a t a    C l e a n i n g

    This was easily the biggest data cleaning project I have experienced. The original data set was seperated by individual year from 2002 - 2023, with each year using different formatting, different header layouts and different columns.

    There were multiple variations of time formats in the original dataset that I needed to transform in to a standardized and uniform format. We see "12:00 p.m.", "noon", "12 noon" amongst other variations. I started by using the split function to seperate any columns that had date and time in the same cell. I would then like to set all times to the 24hr clock (hh:mm) so all noon dates would be "12:00". I started by using find & replace  to find any cells with "noon" and replace with "12:00". For any cells with "p.m." in, I again used find & replace  to replace "p.m." with "PM". I was then able to change the number format in Excel to hh:mm which converted it to the 24hr clock. The same process was used for cells with "a.m.".

    There were also multiple variations of the date that I needed to transform to a standardized format. We see some dates as "d/m/y", and some as "m/d/y" as well as other variations like "March 04". I would like to set all dates to the "d/m/y". I started by using Text to Columns  to change any "m/d/y" to "d/m/y". For dates that included text such as "March 04", I used Split  to seperate the month and the day. I then used the formula =MONTH(DATEVALUE(cell&"1"))  which turns the month text in to its corresponding number. E.g. "March" was converted to "3". I then rejoined the day, month and year in correct order.

    The Type of Disturbance column had a wide variety of answers that needed to be catergorized. I catergorized each type of disturbance in to one of thirteen catergories based on the Department of Energy's 2023 customer survey. This was great for analyzing the data as the hundreds of different specific answers for disturbances such as weather were now all categorized together. E.g. "Hurricane", "Extreme Weatherstorm", "Heavy rain and snowfall" were now all categrorized as "Weather or Natural Disaster".

    The NERC region columns also needed to be cleaned due to the regions having changed over time between 2002 and 2023. I used find & replace  again to replace the old regions with the updated 2023 regions.

    I also added a new column for Event Duration by calculating the difference between when the power outage began, and when it was restored. I used this data to visualize the average restoration time in each Region and State.



    F i n d i n g s

    Weather or Natural Disaster  is the cause of 46% of all power outages in the US. This goes up to a staggering 96% with large power outages that affect over 50,000 people. This is clearly the biggest problem to the Department of Energy. Although you can not control the weather, they could integrate a more weather resistant system. An underground electrical system would go a long way in mitigating the power outages caused by weather.

    The total Megawatts Loss comes in at 1.04 million megawatts, this highlight the need to upgrade the current system to one that reduces this amount of electricity that is going to waste. The majority of Megawatts Loss has come from SERC (374k) and WECC (312k), which suggests these region's infrastructure may be the most outdated.

    The biggest States Affected were California, Texas and Michigan. These should be the highest priority for the Department of Energy. All 3 states were in the top 9 states in Number of Customers Affected, Number of Power Outages, Amount of Megawatts Loss and Average Restoration Time. This geographical data is good for identifying weak spots where the Department of Energy may want to focus their resources.

    The average Restoration Time was a good display of how the response to power outages has improved over time. The Average in 2002 was 69 hours, where as in 2023 that had dropped down to an average of just 8 hours for a power outage to be restored. This shows how the regions have been able to respond to the power outages more efficiently.
